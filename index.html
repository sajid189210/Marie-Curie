About Marie

Marie Skłodowska Curie (1867-1934) was a Polish and naturalized-French physicist and chemist who conducted pioneering research on radioactivity.

She was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize, the first and only woman to win the Nobel Prize twice, and the only person to win the Nobel Prize in two scientific fields. She was also the first woman to become a professor at the University of Paris.

Her achievements included the development of the theory of radioactivity (a term she coined), techniques for isolating radioactive isotopes, and the discovery of two elements, polonium and radium.

Remarkable Achievements

Discovery of Elements

Discovered two new elements: polonium (named after her native Poland) and radium. Both discoveries were made in 1898 while working with her husband Pierre.

Theory of Radioactivity

Developed the theory of radioactivity (a term she coined) and techniques for isolating radioactive isotopes. She was the first to study the effects of radiation on human cells.

Nobel Prizes

Won Nobel Prize in Physics (1903) and Nobel Prize in Chemistry (1911), becoming the first person to win Nobel Prizes in multiple scientific fields.

Pioneer for Women

Became the first female professor at the University of Paris (Sorbonne) and paved the way for women in science, breaking gender barriers in a male-dominated field.

Radiology Pioneer

During World War I, she developed mobile X-ray units to assist battlefield surgeons. Her work saved countless lives and established the field of radiology.

Research Institutions

Founded the Curie Institutes in Paris and Warsaw, which remain important centers for medical research today. Her legacy continues through these institutions.

Life Timeline

1867

Born as Maria Skłodowska in Warsaw, Poland (then part of the Russian Empire).

1891

Moved to Paris to study physics and mathematics at the University of Paris (Sorbonne).

1893

Earned her master's degree in physics, ranking first in her class.

1894

Met Pierre Curie, a professor in the School of Physics, and married him the following year.

1898

Discovered the elements polonium and radium with her husband Pierre Curie.

1903

Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics alongside Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel.

1906

Pierre died in a street accident. Marie took over his teaching position, becoming the first female professor at the Sorbonne.

1911

Awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for her discovery and study of radium and polonium.

1914-1918

During World War I, developed mobile X-ray units to help treat wounded soldiers on the battlefield.

1934

Died at the age of 66 from aplastic anemia, believed to be caused by her long-term exposure to radiation.
